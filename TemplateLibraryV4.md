## CATEGORY A — Currently Learning
**Fires when:** `roast_targets[]` contains `"Currently Learning: X"`
**Comic truth:** You told the employer you don't know the thing they're hiring you for. In writing. Voluntarily.

---

**CL-01** 💀💀
`"'{COURSE}: Currently Learning' and '{TITLE} Position Required' are in the same document now. One of them arrived unprepared."`
> *v3 was "a very specific thing to include." This version forces the collision directly — the two facts meeting in the same sentence IS the joke.*

**CL-02** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Somewhere a hiring manager just read 'Currently Learning: {COURSE}' on a {TITLE} application and put their coffee down."`

**CL-03** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Shipped {PROJECT}. Listed {COURSE} as Currently Learning. Applied for {TITLE} anyway. The plan is to learn it after getting hired. This plan is in the resume."`

**CL-04** 💀💀
`"Currently Learning: {COURSE}. Currently Applying: {TITLE}. Currently: unaware these two sentences cancel each other out."`
> *v3 ended with "the confidence is doing the heavy lifting" — explicitly flagged. New ending mirrors the format and lands the contradiction without the AI cliché.*

**CL-05** 💀
`"{PROJECT}: shipped. {COURSE}: still loading. The application was submitted during the buffering."`
> *v3: "before either situation resolved" is corporate. "During the buffering" is specific, visual, and implies they knew and sent it anyway.*

**CL-06** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"{PROJECT}: shipped. {COURSE}: currently learning. Interview question: 'Walk me through your experience with {COURSE}.' This is where the plan ends."`

**CL-07** 💀💀
`"'Currently Learning: {COURSE}' translates to: 'I know {COURSE} exists, I've seen the thumbnail of a YouTube tutorial about it, and I believe that counts.' It does not count."`
> *v3 ended with "the recruiter does not speak this dialect." Killed. "Seen the thumbnail" is more specific and funnier than "watched a YouTube video." "It does not count." is a cleaner deadpan finish.*

**CL-08** 💀💀
`"Bro put '{COURSE}: Currently Learning' on a {TITLE} application. The {TITLE} role requires {COURSE}. Bro submitted it anyway. Bro is at peace with this."`
> *v3: "thought no one would notice. Bro is cooked." Predictable. "Bro is at peace with this" is more disturbing and funnier — it implies total awareness with zero concern.*

**CL-09** 💀 *(unchanged)*
`"The job requires {COURSE}. The resume says '{COURSE}: Currently Learning.' What was the plan here? This is a genuine question."`

**CL-10** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Demonstrates exceptional growth mindset by applying for a {TITLE} role while actively learning {COURSE} — the skill the {TITLE} role requires. Open to feedback."`

## CATEGORY B — No Metrics
**Fires when:** deployed project present, zero numbers anywhere near it
**Comic truth:** "Deployed" is the most overloaded word in tech. It means nothing without a number behind it.

---

**NM-01** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"The word 'deployed' is doing an unreasonable amount of work in this sentence."`

**NM-02** 💀💀
`"Shipped {PROJECT}. Users: undisclosed. Revenue: undisclosed. Impact: described as significant. The receipts are in the mail."`
> *v3: "left as an exercise for the recruiter." Killed. "The receipts are in the mail" is slang that lands as a punchline. Short, quotable, shareable.*

**NM-03** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"'{PROJECT}: A full-stack application' is one way to describe a CRUD app with no documented users."`

**NM-04** 💀💀
`"{PROJECT}: deployed. Users: theoretical. The application was submitted anyway. Whatever this is, it is brave."`
> *v3: "the audacity required to submit this resume is, itself, a qualification" — too smooth, AI-phrased. "Users: theoretical" is the real joke. "Whatever this is, it is brave" is unexpected and funnier — it implies classification failure.*

**NM-05** 💀 *(unchanged)*
`"{PROJECT}: shipped. The metrics went somewhere. They are not here."`

**NM-06** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — already excellent)*
`"Imagine being the {PROJECT} server. Deployed. Fully operational. Zero confirmed users. Just the developer. Every day. Checking if it's still up."`

**NM-07** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Projects: {PROJECT}. Users of {PROJECT}: not stated. Revenue from {PROJECT}: not stated. Impact of {PROJECT}: described as significant. '{PROJECT}': stated."`

**NM-08** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"This resume describes {PROJECT} the same way a food blogger describes a meal they didn't finish. Beautifully written. No receipts."`

**NM-09** 💀 *(unchanged)*
`"'{PROJECT}: Built and deployed a full-stack application' is recruiter-speak for 'I have a GitHub link and no analytics.' Both are fine. One is on this resume."`

**NM-10** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Bro deployed {PROJECT}, documented zero metrics, and submitted this to real companies. Bro thought he cooked. The metrics disagree."`

## CATEGORY C — Aspiring Title
**Fires when:** title contains `"Aspiring"` or `"Enthusiast"`
**Comic truth:** You opened your job application by announcing you're not ready for it.

---

**AT-01** 💀💀
`"'Aspiring {TITLE}' is a very honest opening for a document requesting a {TITLE} salary. Most people delete this before sending. This person did not."`
> *v3: "The recruiter will appreciate the transparency." Killed. "Most people delete this before sending. This person did not." implies they saw it, reconsidered, and kept it anyway. That's the real roast.*

**AT-02** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — best in library)*
`"Applying for a {TITLE} role. Title on the resume: 'Aspiring {TITLE}.' This is the job hunting equivalent of showing up to a knife fight holding a drawing of a knife."`

**AT-03** 💀💀
`"Most people delete 'Aspiring' before submitting. This resume kept it. In the header. In bold. The edit history on this document would be very revealing."`
> *v3 ended with "Respect." — explicitly flagged. "The edit history on this document would be very revealing" implies multiple drafts where they tried to delete it, and then kept it. The specificity makes it funnier.*

**AT-04** 💀💀
`"Bro put 'Aspiring {TITLE}' at the top of a {TITLE} job application and hit send. 47 times. Bro is living entirely by his own rules."`
> *v3: "The 47 companies have questions" — flat. "Bro is living entirely by his own rules" is unexpectedly empowering, which makes it a better roast. The praise IS the burn.*

**AT-05** 💀💀
`"Passionate about becoming a {TITLE}. Currently not a {TITLE}. Applying to {TITLE} roles anyway. This is either manifesting or a misunderstanding of how hiring works. The resume does not clarify."`
> *v3: "The recruiter calls it something else." Killed. "The resume does not clarify" is deadpan, accurate, and forces the reader to fill in the blank themselves — which is always funnier.*

**AT-06** 💀💀 *(unchanged — scene is too strong to cut)*
`"Somewhere a recruiter opened this resume, read 'Aspiring {TITLE},' and had to close their laptop for five minutes. They came back. They read the rest. They're still processing."`

**AT-07** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — already top tier)*
`"'Aspiring {TITLE}' is the professional equivalent of writing 'aspiring adult' on your driver's license application. The goal is understood. The timing is the issue."`

**AT-08** 💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Job title: Aspiring {TITLE}. Position applied for: {TITLE}. Gap between these two facts: the entire interview process."`

**AT-09** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"Step 1: Become a {TITLE}. Step 2: Get hired as a {TITLE}. This resume skipped Step 1 and went directly to Step 2. The steps were not optional."`

**AT-10** 💀💀 *(unchanged — strong storytelling device)*
`"This resume opens with 'Aspiring {TITLE}.' Every sentence after that is trying to climb out of those two words. Some of them make it. Most don't."`

## CATEGORY D — Skills Overload
**Fires when:** skills section lists 8+ technologies with thin project evidence
**Comic truth:** Listing a technology is not the same as knowing it. The projects section knows this.

---

**SO-01** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — already devastating)*
`"The skills section reads like a Google search history. The projects section reads like a different resume."`

**SO-02** 💀💀 *(unchanged — "watch later queue" is perfect)*
`"'Proficient in {SKILL}' appears on this resume. On this resume, 'proficient' means completed 60% of a YouTube tutorial. The other 40% is still in the watch later queue."`

**SO-03** 💀💀
`"Twelve technologies listed. One project shipped. The gap between those two numbers has a name and it is this resume."`
> *v3 opened with "The skills section is ambitious" — corporate and safe. Numbers are more devastating. "Twelve vs. one" does the work.*

**SO-04** 💀💀
`"Listed {SKILL} alongside approximately everything else. The skills section uses 'familiar with' like punctuation. 'Familiar with' is not a skill level. It is a Yelp review."`
> *v3 ended with "doing a lot of structural work" — flagged. "It is a Yelp review" is unexpected and visual. You wouldn't put a Yelp review on a resume. But here we are.*

**SO-05** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"The skills section is not a skills section. It is a Wikipedia page for technologies this person has heard of."`

**SO-06** 💀💀
`"React: listed. Node.js: listed. Python: listed. Django: listed. AWS: listed. Docker: listed. {PROJECT}: deployed. That last entry is from a different document."`
> *v3: "These two lists are not about the same person." Decent but flat. "That last entry is from a different document" is more specific — it implies the projects section was accidentally copied from someone else's resume. Funnier image.*

**SO-07** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — "prayer list" is the best line in the library)*
`"Bro listed 12 technologies on a resume and proved 2 of them. Bro thinks this is a skills section. This is a prayer list."`

**SO-08** 💀💀
`"'Familiar with {SKILL}' translates to: 'I Googled {SKILL} once, bookmarked the Stack Overflow answer, and felt ready.' The interviewer is also familiar. With this."`
> *v3 ended with "This translation does not appear on the resume." Flat. The new ending mirrors "familiar with" back at the resume ironically — the interviewer is familiar with THIS dialect. The double meaning lands.*

**SO-09** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*
`"This resume lists {SKILL} under Skills and lists {PROJECT} under Projects. {PROJECT} does not use {SKILL}. This is called aspirational alignment. It is not a technical term."`

**SO-10** 💀💀💀
`"The skills section and the projects section have never met. The interview will be the first time they are introduced to each other. This will be uncomfortable for everyone."`
> *v3 had two recruiter references in one template. Killed. Personifying the skills section and projects section as strangers meeting at the interview is absurd, accurate, and genuinely shareable.*

**Comic truth:** The name is doing all the work. The metrics are not helping.

**SC-02** 💀 *(unchanged)*

**SC-03** 💀
`"'{PROJECT}': deployed. Zero users documented. The name alone is doing a TED talk."`
> *v3: "The name is working harder than the metrics" — flagged "working harder" pattern. "The name alone is doing a TED talk" is more specific, visual, and unexpected.*

**SC-04** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*

**SC-05** 💀💀 *(unchanged — "They have moved on." is excellent)*

**SC-06** 💀💀 *(unchanged)*

**SC-07** 💀 *(unchanged — "The branding is excellent." is good damning with faint praise)*
`"'{PROJECT}' has been launched with startup energy, documented with startup language, and shipped to zero confirmed users. The branding is excellent."`

**SC-08** 💀 *(unchanged)*

**SC-09** 💀💀 *(unchanged — meta punchline works)*

**SC-10** 💀💀 *(unchanged — "The dream is alive. The data is not." is quotable)*
`"Bro named their side project '{PROJECT},' documented no users, and listed it as a career highlight. The dream is alive. The data is not."`

**Fires when:** no single category matches cleanly, OR multiple targets combine into something stronger
**Comic truth:** Two honest facts placed next to each other become a third, funnier fact.

---

**CA-01** 💀💀 *(unchanged — "listed voluntarily" is excellent)*
`"'{TARGET_1}' appears on this resume. It was not discovered during a background check. It was listed voluntarily."`

**CA-02** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — "loading screen" is perfect)*
`"{PROJECT}: shipped. {COURSE}: in progress. {TITLE}: current status. This resume is a loading screen."`

**CA-03** 💀💀
`"{PROJECT}: shipped. {COURSE}: still buffering. This is the resume of someone who looked at an incomplete timeline and said 'close enough.'"`
> *v3: "We respect the commitment." — Respect. variant, flagged. "Said 'close enough'" implies the attitude was deliberate, which is funnier and more specific.*

**CA-04** 💀💀
`"This resume was submitted to real companies by a real person who looked at it, nodded, and pressed send. That moment happened. We cannot take it back."`
> *v3: "The confidence is the most impressive line item" — explicitly flagged. The new version is a scene. The "we cannot take it back" finality is devastating and funny because it sounds like regret on behalf of everyone.*

**CA-05** 💀 *(unchanged — keep with flag below)*
`"The skills section lists the tools. The projects section lists {PROJECT}. The cover letter, presumably, bridges the gap. There is no cover letter."`
> ⚠️ **Implementation flag:** "There is no cover letter" only works if Stage 1 confirms no cover letter was detected. If this can't be verified, default to CA-01 or CA-07 instead. Do not fire this template speculatively.

**CA-06** 💀💀

**CA-08** 💀💀
`"This is not a professional document. This is a mood board. {PROJECT} is on it. {COURSE} is on it. Someone formatted it as a PDF and called it done."`
> *v3: "The recruiter's definition of 'professional document' is different from this." Killed. "Formatted it as a PDF and called it done" is specific, visual, and the most accurate possible summary of this kind of resume.*

**CA-09** 💀💀💀 *(unchanged — LinkedIn satire, "Open to relocation." kicker is excellent)*
`"Excited to leverage synergistic growth opportunities while Currently Learning {COURSE} and having Deployed {PROJECT} with no documented impact. Open to relocation."`

**CA-10** 💀💀 *(unchanged — "the recruiters have eyes" is perfect deadpan)*
`"This resume has {PROJECT} in the projects section and {COURSE} in the learning section. It has been sent to recruiters. The recruiters have eyes."`

## CATEGORY G — Generic Summary ⚡ NEW
**Fires when:** resume summary contains empty corporate phrases: "passionate developer," "results-driven," "leverage," "innovative solutions," "dynamic environment," "make an impact," "strong communication skills," or any summary that could apply to 1 million other resumes
**Comic truth:** The first thing you wrote was the least specific thing you've ever said. It was supposed to be the hook.

**Slots used:** `{TITLE}` `{PROJECT}` `{COURSE}` (when available, to connect the roast back to actual resume content)

---

**GS-01** 💀💀
`"The summary opens with 'Passionate developer seeking to leverage innovative solutions.' This sentence has appeared on approximately four million resumes. It is on this one too."`

**GS-02** 💀💀
`"'Passionate about technology.' Cool. Specific. Memorable. This is the opening sentence of a document sent to real companies requesting a real salary."`
> *The caesura rhythm — Cool. Specific. Memorable. — mimics social media praise before the reveal that none of it applies.*

**GS-03** 💀💀
`"The summary section is 47 words. None of them are differentiating. All of them are true of every other resume in the same inbox. This is the first impression."`
> *"The first impression." as a final sentence, alone, is the gut punch.*

**GS-04** 💀💀💀
`"'Results-driven developer with a passion for innovation and a track record of delivering impactful solutions.' The track record is {PROJECT}. The innovation is Currently Learning {COURSE}."`
> *This one pulls the actual resume content into the summary roast — the summary claims are directly contradicted by the specific facts we've already extracted. Extremely shareable.*

**GS-05** 💀💀
`"The summary says 'eager to make a meaningful impact in a dynamic environment.' Zero resumes define 'meaningful.' Zero resumes define 'dynamic.' This one also does not."`

**GS-06** 💀
`"This resume summary could describe 800,000 other developers. It has. By all of them. Simultaneously. None of them knew."`
> *"None of them knew" — the collective unawareness is the darkest possible image here.*

**GS-07** 💀💀
`"'Passionate problem-solver committed to excellence.' Committed enough to write four words about themselves and consider the personal brand complete."`

**GS-08** 💀💀
`"The summary was the first chance to say something specific and true. 'Passionate developer seeking to leverage cutting-edge technologies in a collaborative environment' was the choice that was made."`
> *"was the choice that was made." — the passive voice implies it was deliberate, which is worse.*

**GS-09** 💀💀
`"'Dedicated software developer with strong communication skills.' The strong communication skills have communicated nothing specific in 47 words. The rest of the resume is getting nervous."`
> *"The rest of the resume is getting nervous" — personifying the resume sections as watching the summary fail is unexpected and funny.*

**GS-10** 💀💀
`"The summary section was supposed to be the hook. It is four sentences of warm water. Everything interesting about this resume starts after it. The summary is not aware of this."`
> *"The summary is not aware of this." — giving the summary section its own tragic obliviousness is the right absurdist finish.*

## Implementation Notes for route.ts

### New Category G — Trigger Detection
Stage 1 needs to flag this. Detection string options (any match fires GS):
```
"passionate developer" | "results-driven" | "leverage" | "innovative solutions"
"dynamic environment" | "make an impact" | "strong communication skills"
"committed to excellence" | "problem-solver" | "dedicated developer"
```
Alternatively: if summary word count > 30 AND no specific numbers/projects/titles appear in the summary → fire GS.

### CA-05 Warning
Only fire if Stage 1 explicitly confirms no cover letter was detected. Otherwise skip to next candidate in Catchall category.

### GS-04 Slot Dependency
GS-04 requires both `{PROJECT}` and `{COURSE}` to be populated. If either is null, fall back to GS-01 or GS-08 (no slot dependency).
